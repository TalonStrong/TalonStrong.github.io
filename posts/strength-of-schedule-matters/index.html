The talk around college football during the playoff era even when it was just 4 teams was how much does strength of schedule matter.

Fans, coaches, and analysts constantly argue about how much it should matter when judging a team. A hard schedule can reveal who is actually strong and battle tested, while an easy schedule can boost a team’s records without proving anything during the course of a season. After a full season, strength of schedule is the difference between teams that look similar on paper.

But what I really want to know is how much does strength of schedule matter in terms of winning? Weather you’re a vastly talented team or a team with no talent, isn’t it just about playing who’s in front of you rather then their ranking? Does having a strong strength of schedule matter at the beginning of the season before anyone has played the games?

These were the top 12 teams with the hardest strength of schedule at the end of the 2024 college football season. 6 of these teams were in the Big Ten and 6 were in the SEC. Is that really surprising to anyone? No? Me either, because those are the 2 best conferences in college football and will be for the foreseeable future. With those 2 conferences being the best that means teams in those conferences will have stronger strength of schedules and records.

Some of those teams in the top 12 had abysmal records, including Purdue and Mississippi State who finished with a combined 3-21 record and didn’t win a conference game. Now those teams aren’t the most talented and you can see why they struggled but what about teams with a weaker strength of schedule in weaker conferences.

I’ve discussed the top 12 teams in terms of strength of schedule and how they were in the 2 best conferences but what about teams that aren’t in those juggernaut conferences? For those teams outside the Big Ten and SEC, strength of schedule becomes a different conversation. Lower conferences can often play weaker overall competition, which means their records can look better on the surface when they win. What becomes an interesting conversation is that some of these programs still lose and lose often even with a weaker schedule.

Tulsa is in the American Conference and had one of the lower strength of schedule ratings yet they went 3-9 in 2024. Teams in the middle of the rating system like North Carolina and Arizona state had different seasons all together. North Carolina in the ACC went 6-7 and Arizona State won the Big 12 and had a first round bye in the College Football Playoff.

Strength of schedule and opponent win percentage obviously can have an impact on winning and losing, but they are not perfect or the only indicators of a team’s record. Team’s facing stronger opponents usually makes the season in whole more demanding and challenging. Those teams with tougher schedules will often take more of those losses because every week is always a test or battle. At the same time, this do not explain everything. Every season teams with high opponent win percentages or stronger strength of schedule still find ways to win and sometimes go undefeated. They finish strong because they have better talent, coaching, and depth.

The teams with easier schedules and lower opponent win percentage still struggle because they lack the fundamentals of a good team. So all in all strength of schedule does matter in terms of a teams ceiling and floor, it is absolute not a direct correlation. There is so much more that goes into then that but it can always benefits a team in terms of wins and losses.
